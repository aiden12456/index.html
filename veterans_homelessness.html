Homelessness Among Veterans in America

Veterans risked their lives in service. Yet thousands sleep on sidewalks, in shelters, or in cars. This site explains why this injustice matters, what’s being done, and how you can help.

Why Should We Care About Veteran Homelessness?

This is more than a housing problem. It’s about human dignity, public health, economic waste, and how a nation honors its promises to those who served.

Ethical & Moral Responsibilities

Debt of gratitude Human dignity

Veterans put their lives on the line and sacrificed time, health, and family stability in service to the country. Allowing them to sleep on the streets violates a basic moral obligation to those who served.

Homelessness strips people of shelter, safety, food, healthcare, and stability. These are fundamental human needs. When veterans cannot meet them, their basic rights and dignity are being denied.

National Security & Public Trust

Stability Public safety Credibility

Veterans living on the streets face higher risks of illness, injury, and mental health crises. These crises can spill over into communities if left unaddressed. Housing and support services protect both veterans and the public.

How a country treats its veterans sends a powerful message to current service members, allies, and the world. Failing those who served undermines trust in public institutions and national values.

Economic & Societal Efficiency

Cost of inaction Workforce potential

Chronic homelessness is expensive. It leads to frequent use of emergency rooms, crisis services, shelters, and policing—costs that fall on taxpayers and strained public systems.

Veterans possess valuable skills in leadership, teamwork, and technical fields. With stable housing, healthcare, and job support, many can re-enter the workforce and strengthen the economy rather than being pushed to the margins.

Public Health & Community Strength

Mental health Substance use Social cohesion

Homelessness is closely tied to untreated mental health conditions and substance use disorders. Providing stable housing and accessible care can reduce crises, emergency visits, and preventable suffering like hypothermia or malnutrition.

When communities rally to support veterans—through housing initiatives, outreach, and advocacy—it builds trust, compassion, and civic pride. Showing up for those who served models the kind of society we want to be.

Policy & Practical Considerations

Proven solutions Targeted investments Housing first

Veteran homelessness is solvable. Programs like rapid rehousing, transitional housing, permanent supportive housing, case management, healthcare access, and job training have already shown success in communities across the country.

Smart, targeted investments—especially Housing First, mental health and substance use treatment, and employment assistance—often cost less in the long run than doing nothing. They move veterans from crisis and survival to stability and contribution.

Who Is Affected?

Veteran homelessness is shaped by disability, age, race, mental health, and where people live. Understanding who is most affected helps target solutions where they’re needed most.

Share of the Homeless Population

Veterans make up 5.3% of all homeless adults and 20% of the male homeless population.

Where Veterans Live

68% of homeless veterans live in principal cities, while 32% live in suburban or rural areas—showing this is both an urban and rural issue.

Disability & Health

51% of individual homeless veterans have disabilities, about 50% have serious mental illness, and 70% struggle with substance abuse problems.

Race & Gender

Around 51% of homeless veterans are white males, compared to 38% among non-veteran homeless adults. Race, gender, and service experiences all shape risk.

Age

About 50% of homeless veterans are age 51 or older, compared to 19% of non-veterans—meaning many are aging into homelessness.

These numbers represent people who served their country. Targeted, age- and disability-informed services are essential to meet their needs.

What Is Being Done About This Issue?

Federal agencies, nonprofits, and community-based “veterans helping veterans” programs are working together to prevent and end veteran homelessness—often with promising results.

Meeting the Full Range of Needs

Housing Health Employment

Homeless veterans need more than a bed for the night. Effective responses provide:

  • Secure housing and regular access to nutritional meals.
  • Physical healthcare, mental health counseling, and substance use treatment and aftercare.
  • Personal development, empowerment, and community—often through peer support and veteran-led spaces.
  • Job assessment, training, and placement assistance so veterans can obtain and sustain gainful employment.

The National Coalition for Homeless Veterans (NCHV) emphasizes that employment and income are crucial. Without them, veterans struggle to maintain safe, permanent housing.

Housing & Financial Assistance

HUD–VASH VA grants Prevention
  • HUD–VASH Program: A partnership between the U.S. Department of Housing and Urban Development (HUD) and the VA that provides permanent housing vouchers to homeless veterans and their families, paired with VA case management and supportive services.
  • VA Grants: The VA funds organizations nationwide to help veterans access housing, healthcare, financial planning, and other supports.
  • Supportive Services for Veteran Families (SSVF): A preventative program that helps veterans at risk of homelessness stay housed or move quickly into stable housing.

Outreach & Community-Based Programs

Outreach Veterans helping veterans
  • Health Care for Homeless Veterans and the National Call Center for Homeless Veterans serve as front doors to VA homeless programs and the broader VA healthcare system.
  • New nationwide initiatives like “Getting Veterans Off the Street” use targeted outreach to connect unsheltered veterans with housing, healthcare, and case management.
  • Community-based, nonprofit, “veterans helping veterans” models are especially effective. Transitional housing that offers structure, sobriety, and camaraderie with fellow veterans helps people regain control of their lives.

Because government funding is limited and VA services reach less than half of all homeless veterans, local community groups play a critical role in filling the gaps.

Strategy: Housing First & Long-Term Stability

Housing first Rapid rehousing Supportive housing

Many communities now use a Housing First approach: provide permanent, safe housing as the starting point, then layer on services like mental health care, substance use treatment, and job support.

Rapid rehousing, permanent supportive housing, and blended models of case management and peer support have shown that, with the right investment, veteran homelessness can be dramatically reduced—often faster and more cost-effectively than people expect.

How You Can Help

You don’t need to be an expert to make a difference. Small, consistent actions—local volunteering, donations, and advocacy—can help move veterans from homelessness to stability.

Individual Actions

Volunteer Donate Support
  • Support veterans’ housing and healthcare initiatives. Look for local veteran shelters, transitional housing, and Housing First projects.
  • Volunteer with veteran service organizations, VA hospitals, community outreach teams, or housing and job programs.
  • Donate time or skills—legal aid, job coaching, résumé help, mental health services, or healthcare can be life-changing.
  • Give financially, even in small amounts, to trusted nonprofits that focus on veteran homelessness and housing.

Civic & Policy Actions

Advocacy Policy Coalitions
  • Advocate for funding and policies that address veteran homelessness at the local, state, and federal levels—especially Housing First, mental health services, and job training.
  • Vote with veterans and housing policy in mind. Contact representatives about veteran housing programs and oversight.
  • Attend town halls or city council meetings to support veteran-specific housing and outreach programs.
  • Join or form local coalitions that bring together VA offices, housing authorities, nonprofits, faith communities, and veterans’ groups to coordinate services.
